Processing of Materials

Design of a Credit/Debit Card

Since credit cards are used in many large transactions, and contain personal information about their owners, they must have many security precautions. There are many key design pieces:

  1. The card's account number
    • This is the piece of information needed to complete a transaction
    • Therefore, this information must be protected
    • Each customer has their own unique number, each company has a different length, from 13 to 20 numbers, allowing for one billion unique numbers
    • The unique number of a credit card makes it difficult to commit fraud
  2. The signature panel
    • The owner's signature documents the owner's handwriting
    • The panel has a specail erasure feature that documents finger prints
  3. A magnetic stripe
    • The magnetic stripe is coated in particles of iron oxide that contain binary data
    • The information that is stored on these stripes is not known to the general public, because companies do not want it known

Production of Raw Materials

The primary manufacturing material of credit/debit cards is polyvinyl chloride. PVC is mainly exported from the United States, 33 billion tons are produced each year. PVC is processed from ethylene dichloride, which is derived from combining chlorine (from sea salt) and ethylene, which comes from petroleum. The ethylene dichloride is them decomposed by a high amount of heat and is then polymerized to create PVC.

Plastic Compounding & Molding

The core of a credit card is made by melting and stirring polyvinyl chloride acetate with other preservatives. The mixed ingredients are moved to an extrusion molding apparatus that makes the molten plastic move into a flat opening, or orifice, also known as a die, and as the sheet emerges from the press it goes through a series of three rollers stacked on each other and keep the sheet a proper thickness. The sheets then go through more cooling units and then are cut into sheets by saws, shears or hot wires. The sliced sheets then enter a stacker that organizes them into the appropriate piles for subsequent operations.

Printing

The plastic core of the card needs to be printed with texts and graphics. this proces is done with multiple silkscreen processes. Additionally, One of the laminate films might also go through more operations where it is printed with magnetic ink. The magnetic strip then can be joined by a hot stamping press. After printing, the core is ready to be laminated.

Lamination

Lamination protects the card and increases it's strength. The core is sent through a system of rollers; laminate is stocked above and below the card. The laminates are then sent into the vacuum along with the core; the vacuum holds the three pieces of plastic together as they travel to a tacking station. At the tacking station, quartz infrared heat lamps warm both layers of the laminate. The lamps have reflectors that direct and focus their energy onto a narrow area which allows for the cleanest bonding of the two materials. They are fully bonded with metal platens that have a heat of 266 degrees Fahrenheit/130 degrees Celsius and are pressed into the card with a pressure of 166 psi/square inch; the process can take 3 minutes.

Die cutting and Embossing

After lamination, the sheets are cut by assemblies. Each assembly gets a sheet which is cut into 63 credit cards. Then, the cards are embossed with account numbers and prepared for shipping, often attached to a paper letter with an adhesive
